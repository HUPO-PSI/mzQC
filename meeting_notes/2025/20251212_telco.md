## Current QC Practices in Steffen Neumann's Lab (GC–MS and LC–MS)

- QC is currently largely manual and operator-driven, with qualitative "go / no-go" decisions before running valuable samples.
- GC–MS QC setup:
    - QC sample consists of a mixture of 10 authentic standards.
    - Standards are selected for:
        - Chemical stability
        - Coverage across the retention time range
        - Coverage of relevant m/z range.
    - Metrics manually checked:
        - Retention time stability
        - Peak shape (via EIC inspection)
        - Signal intensity (detector degradation, sensitivity loss).
    - Planned addition (next year):
        - Chromatographic resolution as an explicit QC metric (noted as important from a chemist's perspective but currently underused in practice).
- LC–MS QC setup:
    - Also uses a 10-compound mixture.
    - Metrics manually checked:
        - Retention time
        - Peak shape
        - Signal intensity
        - Mass accuracy
        - System pressure (reported by LC system, not present in mzML).
    - Chromatographic resolution also planned for inclusion next year.
- QC sample usage:
    - QC samples are run before, during, and after experiments.
    - These QC runs are not yet systematically integrated into automated or standardized QC evaluation pipelines.
    - Potential exists for multi-run QC metrics (e.g., retention time stability across an experiment).
- Data recording:
    - Decisions and interventions (e.g., cleaning instruments) are logged as free text in an electronic lab notebook.
    - QC outcomes are not stored as structured, quantitative metrics.
- Automation goals:
    - Strong interest in automated extraction of QC metrics, generation of mzQC files, and visualization via dashboards.
    - Preference for quantitative QC metrics with thresholding (e.g., green/orange/red zones) rather than qualitative inspection.

Automation Challenges and Infrastructure Constraints

- Vendor heterogeneity is a major challenge:
    - Different raw file formats across vendors.
    - Pressure and other instrument-level metadata often not included in mzML.
- IT and network constraints:
    - Instrument PCs are highly isolated (Windows 95 / Windows 7 era machines, restricted network access).
    - Data are copied to a limited network storage "handover" area.
    - Direct installation and maintenance of complex software stacks on acquisition PCs is undesirable.
- Preferred solutions:
    - Minimal-footprint deployments (single executables where possible).
    - Avoidance of managing multiple R/Python environments on acquisition machines.
    - Interest in containerized solutions:
        - Docker preferred conceptually, but not feasible on network storage.
        - Singularity required due to root access and root-squash constraints.
        - Current Singularity environment suffers from version fragmentation, requiring cleanup.
- Conversion considerations:
    - mzML conversion could be done:
        - On acquisition PCs (simpler if ProteoWizard is installed), or
        - On Linux side after copying raw files (advantage: smaller raw files → faster transfer).
    - ProteoWizard / msconvert:
        - Thermo RAW parsing is now feasible on Linux (mono or native RAW file parser).
        - Less of a blocker than in previous years.
- Deployment tooling:
    - Steffen mentioned Riddle as a promising solution:
    - Can bundle R dependencies into a single executable.
    - Can optionally expose a lightweight Shiny-based UI.
    - Suitable for deployment on measurement PCs.

## QC Metrics Categorization – Ontology Design Discussion

- Wout presented a new multi-dimensional QC metrics categorization, developed during the ELIXIR BioHackathon.
- Chris provided detailed review comments, focusing on:
    - Redundancy between dimensions (e.g., analytical dimension vs. workflow stage).
    - Orthogonality of categories.
- Key tensions identified:
    - Analytical dimension (e.g., mass accuracy, chromatographic performance) overlaps strongly with workflow stages (LC, MS1, MS2).
    - Some concepts (e.g., fragmentation efficiency, mass measurement) appear in multiple dimensions.
    - Ion mobility and LC requirements blur boundaries further.
- Analytical dimension identified as critical:
    - It is the only dimension shown by default in ontology viewers.
    - Needs to be high-level, non-redundant, and meaningful.
- Design philosophy discussion:
    - Avoid "catch-all" categories that encourage misuse.
    - Balance between:
        - Being specific enough to be informative
        - Being general enough to avoid constant ontology extensions.
        - A completely closed system may not be realistic, but extensions should be infrequent and justified.
- Proposed way forward:
    - Reduce analytical dimension to a smaller set of agreed-upon categories.
    - Apply proposed categorization retrospectively to existing QC metrics (~100) to identify gaps or inconsistencies.
    - Seek broader community feedback (lightweight PSI-style consultation).
- Action points:
    - **Wout & Chris:** Refine analytical dimension categories.
    - **Working group:** Test categorization against existing metrics.
    - **Community:** Provide feedback once a refined proposal is shared.
